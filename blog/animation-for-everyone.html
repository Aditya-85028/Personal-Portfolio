Why Animation is for Everyone

March 22, 2024 Industry Thoughts 6 min read

Animation has long been misunderstood as a medium exclusively for children. This misconception has prevented many adults from experiencing some of the most profound, innovative, and emotionally resonant storytelling in cinema. It's time to challenge this outdated perspective.

The Misconception of "Just for Kids"

The association between animation and children's entertainment stems from the early days of Disney's dominance in the industry. While Disney's family-friendly approach has created beloved classics, it has also created a narrow perception of what animation can be. This view ignores the rich history of animated films that tackle mature themes, complex narratives, and sophisticated visual storytelling.

Animation as a Medium for Complex Themes

Animation has the unique ability to explore themes that live-action films often struggle with:

  • Existential questions: Films like Soul and Inside Out explore consciousness, purpose, and the human condition
  • Social commentary: Zootopia addresses prejudice and systemic discrimination
  • Environmental concerns: Studio Ghibli films like Princess Mononoke tackle complex ecological themes
  • Mental health: Inside Out provides a nuanced exploration of emotions and depression
  • War and conflict: Grave of the Fireflies portrays the devastating impact of war on civilians

The Visual Language of Animation

Animation offers filmmakers complete control over every visual element, allowing for:

  • Metaphorical storytelling: Abstract concepts can be visualized in ways impossible in live-action
  • Stylized reality: Artists can create worlds that reflect emotional states rather than physical reality
  • Unlimited possibilities: No constraints of physics, budget, or practical effects
  • Consistent visual style: Every frame can be crafted to serve the story's emotional core

Breaking Down Age Barriers

The best animated films work on multiple levels, appealing to different age groups simultaneously:

  • Children: Enjoy the colorful visuals, humor, and adventure
  • Teens: Connect with coming-of-age themes and identity exploration
  • Adults: Appreciate the deeper themes, cultural references, and sophisticated storytelling
  • Elderly: Find comfort in nostalgia and wisdom in life lessons

Cultural and Artistic Significance

Animation has produced some of the most culturally significant films of our time:

  • Cultural preservation: Films like Coco celebrate and preserve cultural traditions
  • Artistic innovation: Spider-Man: Into the Spider-Verse revolutionized visual storytelling
  • Global perspectives: International animation brings diverse cultural viewpoints to global audiences
  • Social progress: Animated films often lead the way in representation and inclusivity

The Future of Animation

As technology advances, animation is becoming more accessible and diverse:

  • Independent creators: New tools enable individual artists to create feature-quality films
  • Diverse voices: More creators from different backgrounds are entering the field
  • Experimental techniques: Mixed media and innovative approaches are expanding the medium
  • Adult-oriented content: Streaming platforms are supporting more mature animated content

How to Approach Animation as an Adult

If you're new to animation or hesitant to try it, here are some suggestions:

  • Start with critically acclaimed films: Look for films that have received recognition beyond children's categories
  • Explore different styles: From hand-drawn to computer-generated, each style offers unique experiences
  • Read reviews and analysis: Understanding the context can enhance your appreciation
  • Watch with an open mind: Let go of preconceptions about what animation "should" be
  • Discuss with others: Share your thoughts and discover new perspectives

Conclusion

Animation is not a genre—it's a medium. Like literature, painting, or music, it can be used to tell any type of story, explore any theme, and reach any audience. The misconception that animation is only for children limits both creators and audiences from experiencing some of the most innovative and meaningful storytelling in cinema.

The next time you see an animated film, approach it with the same openness you would bring to any other form of art. You might be surprised by what you discover—not just about the film, but about yourself and the world around you.
